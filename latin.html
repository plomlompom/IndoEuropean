    Late Proto-Indo-European eh₂ (feminine) declension

    Possibly after the split-off of the Anatolian branch, an explicitely feminine declension emerges, marked by a -eh₂- stem suffix to which athematic endings are appended (Beekes 2011 p. 189; Fortson 2010 p. 132-133; Meier-Brügger 2010 p. 323-324, 334-335, 421; Meiser 2002 p. 131; Sihler 1995 p. 245, 266). The singular vocative is a special case, with different theories about its form and the logic behind it.

    Proto-Italic loss of laryngeals, and Proto-Indo-European laryngeal coloring

    The laryngeals disappear. Vowels neighboring lost laryngeals are left in their phonetic laryngeal coloring (i.e. -e- as -a- when neighbor to -h₂-), and lengthened if the lost laryngeal came after and was word-final or followed by a consonant (Beekes 2011 p. 149-150; Clackson/Horrocks 2007 p. 9-10; Fortson 2010 p. 63-64; Meier-Brügger 2010 p. 244; Meiser 2002 p. 28-30, 56-57; Sihler 1995 p. 42-50). Word-final post-consonantal laryngeals become -a (Sihler 1995 p. 99; Meiser 2002 p. 107). From these changes, vowel neighborships -aa- (a-stem dative singular), -āā- (a-stem instrumental singular) and (possibly with the a-stem genitive/ablative singular) -ao- emerge that contract to -ā- (Meiser 2002 p. 87-88; Sihler 1995 p. 83). Post-laryngeal continuation of former eh₂-stem endings -eh₂bʰi (singular instrumental) and -eh₂su (plural locative) is not directly discussed in the literature, but would have given -ābʰi and -āsu by these rules. The a-stem singular vocative form reflects no single rule path, but an uncertainty that fits the variety of theories about its original form. The i/u-stem singular instrumentals in -ī (<) and -ū (<) are not directly attested for Italic, but are the logical result of the above rules also found in Vedic and Avestan and speculatively included here as such (Fortson 2010 p. 116, Sihler 1995 p. 312, 323).
